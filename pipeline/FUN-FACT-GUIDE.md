# Fun Fact Writing Guide

> **Gold standard.** This guide defines how fun facts should read, feel, and add value after a player answers a question. The Enrichment Agent uses it as its primary reference. Changes require human approval.

---

## 1. Purpose

A fun fact appears after the player answers a question (right or wrong). Its job is to make the answer **memorable and shareable** — the kind of thing someone repeats to a friend the next day.

It is NOT:
- A restatement of the correct answer
- An explanation of why the answer is right (that's the `explanation` field)
- A Wikipedia summary
- A lecture

---

## 2. The Three Qualities

Every fun fact must be:

### Surprising
Contradicts what you'd expect, or reveals something hidden. The reader should think "oh really?" or "I didn't know that."

- Good: "Michelangelo didn't want the job — he considered himself a sculptor, not a painter. Pope Julius II essentially forced him into it."
- Bad: "Michelangelo was an Italian Renaissance artist who painted many famous works."

### Concrete
Specific details, not vague statements. Numbers, names, dates, and vivid images stick in memory.

- Good: "He worked mostly alone on scaffolding 20 metres up for 4 years, and suffered permanent damage to his eyesight."
- Bad: "It took him a long time and was very difficult."

### Connected
Extends what the question already told you. Doesn't go off on a tangent — builds on the answer.

- Good (for "Who painted the Sistine Chapel?"): "He actually painted it standing up, not lying on his back as commonly depicted. He wrote a poem complaining about the pain in his neck."
- Bad: "The Vatican City is the smallest country in the world." (tangent — not about the painting)

---

## 3. Structure

### Length
- **1-2 sentences.** Maximum 3 if the fact genuinely needs it.
- Target: 100-200 characters. Short enough to read in a glance, long enough to be interesting.
- Must work as standalone text (for future SMS/notification use).

### Tone
- Conversational, not encyclopaedic. Write like you're telling a mate at the pub.
- "He didn't even want the job" not "Historical records indicate reluctance on the part of the artist."
- Light surprise or humour is great. Sarcasm and jokes that need explaining are not.

### Opening
- Lead with the surprising part. Don't build up to it.
- Good: "He didn't even want the job — Pope Julius II forced him into it."
- Bad: "Michelangelo was commissioned by Pope Julius II. Interestingly, he didn't actually want to do it."

## 4. What Makes a Great Fun Fact

### Patterns That Work

| Pattern | Example | Why It Works |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Reluctance/irony** | "Einstein failed his university entrance exam on the first try." | Humanises a legend |
| **Surprising number** | "The Great Wall of China is roughly 21,196 km long — enough to stretch halfway around the Earth." | Scale creates a "wow" |
| **Hidden connection** | "The word 'salary' comes from the Latin 'salarium' — Roman soldiers were partly paid in salt." | Links something familiar to something unexpected |
| **Unexpected origin** | "Ketchup originated in 17th century China as a fermented fish sauce called 'ke-tsiap'." | Challenges assumptions |
| **Near-miss / almost didn't happen** | "The Beatles were rejected by Decca Records in 1962. The label said guitar groups were 'on the way out'." | Drama and irony |
| **Vivid detail** | "Cleopatra lived closer in time to the Moon landing than to the building of the Great Pyramid." | Reframes something familiar |
| **Record/extreme** | "Usain Bolt's top speed during his 100m world record was 44.72 km/h — faster than most city speed limits." | Puts achievement in relatable terms |

### Patterns to Avoid

| Pattern | Example | Why It Fails |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Restating the answer** | "Jupiter is indeed the largest planet in our Solar System." | Adds nothing new |
| **Vague praise** | "Shakespeare is widely considered one of the greatest writers of all time." | Everyone knows this already |
| **Unrelated tangent** | For a question about France: "The Eiffel Tower was built for the 1889 World Fair." (if the question was about French cuisine) | Not connected to the question topic |
| **Too technical** | "The Sistine Chapel fresco uses the buon fresco technique with pigments applied to wet lime plaster." | Nobody at a pub cares |
| **Multi-paragraph essay** | Any fact that needs 3+ sentences | Too long for the format |

## 5. Category-Specific Tips

### History
- Focus on the human story behind the event. "Napoleon was actually average height for his era — the 'short' myth came from British propaganda."
- Near-miss and "what if" angles work well.

### Science & Nature
- Put numbers in relatable terms. "A teaspoon of neutron star would weigh about 6 billion tonnes."
- Animal facts are goldmines — people love weird animal behaviour.

### Entertainment
- Behind-the-scenes details. "The famous chest-burster scene in Alien wasn't rehearsed — the cast's horrified reactions were genuine."
- Casting near-misses. "Will Smith turned down the role of Neo in The Matrix."

### Geography
- Comparison facts. "Russia has 11 time zones — when it's breakfast in Kaliningrad, it's dinner in Kamchatka."
- Counterintuitive locations. "Reno, Nevada is further west than Los Angeles."

### Food & Drink
- Origin stories. "Champagne was accidentally invented by monks trying to stop their wine from fizzing."
- Surprising ingredients or processes.

### Sports
- Records and near-misses. Personal stories behind famous moments.

---

## 6. The SMS Test

Every fun fact should pass this test: **if you sent it as a standalone text message with no other context, would the recipient find it interesting?**

This is literally the future use case — a daily "Question of the Day" via SMS where the fun fact follows the answer. If the fact needs the question to make sense, it's too dependent. If it reads well on its own, it's good.

- Passes: "Michelangelo painted the Sistine Chapel standing up, not lying down. He wrote a poem about the neck pain."
- Fails: "That's correct! The answer was indeed Michelangelo." (needs the question for context)
- Passes: "Honey never spoils. Archaeologists found 3,000-year-old honey in Egyptian tombs that was still edible."
- Fails: "Honey has a very long shelf life due to its chemical properties." (boring, no surprise)
